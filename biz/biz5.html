Stock Redemption in Corporate Liquidation — Gain or Dividend?

When a company redeems its own stock during liquidation, the tax outcome for shareholders is not automatic.

The key question is whether the redemption is treated as:

  • a sale or exchange → Capital gain / loss, or
  • a dividend equivalent → Ordinary income

U.S. tax law (IRC §302) provides five qualification tests to determine the correct treatment.

What Is Stock Redemption?

A stock redemption occurs when a corporation acquires its own shares from a shareholder in exchange for cash or property.

In liquidation scenarios, redemption often functions as a partial or complete exit for shareholders.

Default rule:
Stock redemption is treated as a dividend unless one of the §302 qualification tests is met.

The 5 Qualification Tests (IRC §302)

# Qualification Test Core Idea
1 Complete Termination Shareholder fully exits ownership
2 Substantially Disproportionate Ownership % drops significantly
3 Not Essentially Equivalent to a Dividend Meaningful reduction in control
4 Partial Liquidation Corporate contraction
5 Redemption to Pay Death Taxes Estate tax relief exception

Example Scenario

Assume the following facts:

Item Details
Corporation ABC Inc. (C-Corporation)
Shareholder Mr. Kim
Shares owned 1,000 shares (100%)
Tax basis $400,000
Redemption proceeds $1,000,000

Qualification 1: Complete Termination

If ABC Inc. redeems all 1,000 shares and Mr. Kim retains no ownership, the redemption qualifies as a sale or exchange.

Tax result:
Amount realized: $1,000,000
Tax basis: $400,000
Capital gain: $600,000

✔ Gain or loss is recognized under capital gain rules.

Qualification 2: Substantially Disproportionate

Before redemption, Mr. Kim owns 100%. After redemption, his ownership falls below:

  • 50% voting power, and
  • 80% of his prior ownership

If ABC redeems 800 shares and Mr. Kim retains only 200 shares (20%), this test is satisfied.

→ Capital gain treatment applies.

Qualification 3: Not Essentially Equivalent to a Dividend

This test focuses on control and influence, not just percentages.

If the redemption meaningfully reduces the shareholder’s voting rights, courts may allow sale treatment.

⚠️ Highly subjective and frequently litigated.

Qualification 4: Partial Liquidation

If ABC Inc. permanently terminates one of its business lines and distributes cash to shareholders, the redemption may qualify.

In this case, proceeds are treated as a return of capital first, then capital gain.

Qualification 5: Redemption to Pay Death Taxes

Special relief exists when redemption proceeds are used to pay estate taxes.

This applies only under strict conditions and limited amounts.

When Qualification Fails

If none of the five tests are met:

  • Redemption is treated as a dividend
  • No basis recovery
  • Taxed as ordinary income
