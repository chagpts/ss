Estate Tax vs Capital Gain — Why Death Does NOT Automatically Create Gain

When a shareholder dies and the corporation redeems stock, many people instinctively ask:

“Isn’t that a capital gain?”

Surprisingly, the answer is often no. This article explains why estate tax and capital gain tax are fundamentally different, and where IRC §303 fits into stock redemption planning.

1️⃣ Estate Tax and Capital Gain Are Different Taxes

Item Estate Tax Capital Gain Tax
Tax base Total estate value Gain on disposition
Taxpayer Estate Shareholder / heir
Trigger Death Sale or exchange
Code section IRC §2001 IRC §1001 / §302

✔ Death triggers transfer tax, not income tax.

2️⃣ Step-Up in Basis: The Missing Link

At death, most assets receive a step-up in basis to fair market value under IRC §1014.

Before Death After Death
Original basis: $200,000 Stepped-up basis: $1,000,000

This reset in basis explains why many redemptions produce no capital gain at all.

3️⃣ Where Stock Redemption Comes In

A stock redemption occurs when a corporation buys back its own shares.

Under IRC §302, redemptions are treated as dividends by default unless a qualification applies.

4️⃣ IRC §303 — Redemption to Pay Death Taxes

IRC §303 exists to solve a practical problem:

“How can heirs pay estate tax without being hit with dividend income?”

Section 303 allows certain redemptions used to pay estate taxes and funeral expenses to be treated as a sale or exchange, rather than a dividend.

5️⃣ Numerical Example

Item Amount
FMV of stock at death $1,000,000
Stepped-up basis $1,000,000
Estate tax due $400,000
Redemption proceeds $400,000

Tax result:
Amount realized: $400,000
Basis allocated: $400,000
Capital gain: $0

✔ No capital gain — but also no dividend. That is the real value of §303.

6️⃣ When Capital Gain DOES Appear

Capital gain arises only if:

Redemption proceeds > stepped-up basis

Example:

  • Basis: $1,000,000
  • Redemption: $1,200,000

→ Capital gain = $200,000

7️⃣ Key Takeaways

  • Death does not trigger capital gain
  • Estate tax is a transfer tax
  • Redemption defaults to dividend treatment
  • §303 prevents dividend characterization
  • Capital gain depends on basis, not death
