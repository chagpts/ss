Why the Korean Government Cares About the Dollar

Background

Recent news reported that the U.S. revised its proposal regarding South Korea’s $350 billion commitment. This raised concerns in South Korea, especially around dollar demand and exchange rates.

Many Korean companies earn revenue in U.S. dollars through exports and overseas business. Instead of converting those dollars into Korean won, companies often hold onto dollars for future investments or payments.

As more companies keep dollars rather than exchanging them, overall dollar demand increases, pushing the dollar higher and weakening the won.

Corporate Perspective

Why Companies Hold Dollars

  • They need dollars for overseas investments
  • They must make dollar-based payments
  • They want to reduce currency risk

However, this behavior reduces the supply of dollars in the domestic market, making the dollar stronger.

Year-End Financial Pressure

Toward the end of the year, many companies hold large amounts of dollar-denominated debt. If the dollar remains strong, the value of this debt looks larger in won terms.

This can make financial statements look worse and negatively affect key financial ratios. As a result, companies often prefer a lower dollar at year-end.

Government & National Pension Fund (NPS)

The Korean government does not only rely on verbal guidance or direct intervention. It can also use large institutional investors such as the National Pension Service (NPS).

The NPS holds massive overseas assets, most of which are denominated in U.S. dollars. This creates significant exposure to currency risk.

Using Forward Contracts for Currency Hedging

To manage this risk, the NPS often uses currency forward contracts.

In a forward contract, the NPS agrees to sell dollars and buy won at a fixed exchange rate on a future date.

This has two important effects:

  • It helps the NPS protect its portfolio from exchange rate volatility
  • It increases future dollar supply in the market, easing dollar pressure

Why This Matters for the Government

When the NPS actively hedges its dollar exposure using forward contracts, it indirectly supports the government’s goal of stabilizing the exchange rate.

This approach allows the government to influence the market without direct and visible intervention, reducing the risk of criticism from global markets.

Key Debate

This leads to an important question:

Is using public funds like the National Pension for currency hedging a smart stabilization tool, or does it blur the line between investment management and policy intervention?

Discussion Questions

  • Does currency hedging by public funds distort the market?
  • Should institutions like the NPS focus only on returns, or also on economic stability?
  • Would this strategy work the same way in other countries?
