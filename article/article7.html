When Persistence Works — and When It Makes Things Worse

We love stories about people who achieve the impossible. Someone keeps trying despite all odds—and finally succeeds. Persistence is often praised as a virtue. But is continuing always the right choice?

The Power of Doing the “Impossible”

Many breakthroughs happen because someone refused to give up. Athletes, entrepreneurs, and artists often succeed precisely because they kept going when others quit.

In these cases, persistence turns the impossible into the possible. Failure becomes feedback, and effort leads to growth.

When Trying Harder Backfires

However, not every difficult situation improves with more effort. Sometimes, continuing down the same path only makes things worse.

This is known as the sunk cost trap— we keep investing time, energy, or money simply because we have already invested so much.

Knowing When to Stop

The real challenge is not choosing between quitting and persisting, but knowing when to do each.

Walking away is not always failure. Sometimes, it is a strategic decision that prevents further damage and opens the door to better opportunities.

Redefining Success

Success is not just about pushing forward. It is also about reflection, adjustment, and courage— including the courage to change direction.

The wisest choice often lies in understanding the difference between meaningful persistence and harmful stubbornness.

Discussion Questions

  • Do you believe persistence is always a good thing? Why or why not?
  • Can you think of a time when continuing made a situation worse?
  • How do you personally decide when to quit versus when to keep going?
  • Is quitting sometimes a sign of intelligence rather than weakness?
  • How does fear (of failure or regret) affect our decisions?
  • Have you ever stayed in a situation because of “wasted effort”?
  • Do cultural values influence how we view quitting?
  • How can we reframe quitting in a healthier way?
  • What advice would you give someone stuck in a failing situation?
  • What does “success” mean to you now compared to the past?
